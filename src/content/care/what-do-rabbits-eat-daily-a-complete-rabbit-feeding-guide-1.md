## Understanding a Rabbit’s Natural Diet

Rabbits are natural grazers. In the wild, they spend much of their day eating grasses, weeds, and other fibrous plants. Their digestive systems are designed to process small amounts of food continuously, which means domestic rabbits still need regular access to fibre throughout the day.

Because of this, a rabbit’s diet should focus on foods that support healthy digestion and natural chewing behaviour. Feeding too many treats, sugary foods, or low-fibre mixes can quickly lead to obesity, digestive issues, and dental problems.

A balanced daily diet helps rabbits maintain:
- Healthy digestion
- Proper tooth wear
- Stable weight
- Good energy levels
- Healthy behaviour patterns

Getting the diet right early on can prevent many common health issues later in life.

---

## Hay: The Most Important Part of a Rabbit’s Diet

Hay should make up the vast majority of what a rabbit eats every day. In fact, around 80–90% of a healthy rabbit’s diet should consist of fresh hay.

Hay is essential because it provides the fibre rabbits need to keep their digestive systems moving properly. It also helps naturally wear down their teeth, which continuously grow throughout their lives.

Without enough hay, rabbits are at a much higher risk of developing serious problems such as digestive slowdowns or overgrown teeth.

Good quality hay should smell fresh, feel dry, and be free from dust or mould. Popular options for pet rabbits include timothy hay, meadow hay, orchard grass hay, and botanical hay.

Rabbits should always have unlimited access to hay, both during the day and overnight.

---

## Fresh Greens and Vegetables

Alongside hay, rabbits benefit from a variety of fresh leafy greens each day. Vegetables provide additional nutrients, hydration, and enrichment, helping keep feeding time interesting and natural.

Many rabbits enjoy foods such as romaine lettuce, coriander, parsley, basil, rocket, and spring greens. Introducing a variety of safe greens can encourage natural foraging behaviour and help prevent boredom.

However, it is important to introduce new foods gradually. A sudden change in diet can upset a rabbit’s sensitive digestive system, so any new vegetables should be added slowly while monitoring for any changes in appetite or droppings.

Some vegetables, including carrots and kale, are best fed in moderation due to their sugar or calcium content. While safe as occasional treats, they should not become the main part of a rabbit’s daily diet.

---

## Should Rabbits Eat Pellets?

Pellets can play a useful role in a rabbit’s diet, but they should only be given in small measured amounts. Many owners accidentally overfeed pellets, which can reduce hay consumption and lead to weight gain.

A good pellet should be high in fibre and fairly plain in appearance. Colourful muesli-style rabbit foods are generally not recommended, as rabbits tend to pick out the sweeter pieces while leaving behind the healthier ingredients.

The exact amount of pellets a rabbit needs depends on factors such as age, size, breed, and activity level. Younger rabbits often require more pellets while growing, whereas healthy adult rabbits usually only need a small portion each day alongside unlimited hay.

---

## Fresh Water Matters More Than Many Owners Realise

Clean fresh water should always be available. Hydration plays an important role in digestion, especially when rabbits consume large amounts of hay.

Many rabbits prefer drinking from heavy ceramic bowls rather than bottles, as bowls allow them to drink more naturally. Regardless of the method used, water should be changed daily and checked regularly throughout the day.

---

## Foods Rabbits Should Avoid

Although rabbits enjoy exploring different foods, not everything is safe for them to eat. Some foods can upset digestion, while others may be toxic.

Foods that should never be fed to rabbits include:
- Chocolate
- Avocado
- Onions
- Garlic
- Bread
- Dairy products
- Sugary snacks
- Processed human foods

Iceberg lettuce is also best avoided, as it contains very little nutritional value and may cause digestive upset in some rabbits.

When changing any part of a rabbit’s diet, gradual adjustments are always safer than sudden changes.

---

## Feeding Young Rabbits

Baby rabbits have slightly different nutritional needs from adults. Young rabbits typically require more calories and nutrients while growing, which is why pellets are often fed more freely during early development.

Hay remains just as important for young rabbits, helping support healthy digestion and tooth development from an early age.

Fresh vegetables should be introduced slowly and carefully once young rabbits are old enough, allowing their digestive systems time to adjust.

---

## Signs of a Healthy Diet

A rabbit eating a healthy, balanced diet will usually appear active, alert, and maintain a healthy body condition. One of the easiest ways to monitor rabbit health is by observing eating habits and droppings.

Healthy rabbits should:
- Eat hay regularly throughout the day
- Produce consistent droppings
- Maintain steady energy levels
- Show interest in food
- Keep a healthy weight

A rabbit refusing food, especially hay, can quickly become a medical emergency and should never be ignored.

---

## Final Thoughts

Feeding rabbits properly does not need to be complicated, but consistency is important. Unlimited hay, fresh leafy greens, clean water, and controlled pellet portions create the foundation of a healthy rabbit diet.

By understanding what rabbits naturally need to eat, owners can help support better digestion, healthier teeth, and a happier life overall.
