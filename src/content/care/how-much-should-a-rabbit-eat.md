## Understanding How Rabbits Naturally Eat

One of the most important things to understand about rabbits is that they are natural grazers. Unlike humans or many other pets, rabbits are not designed to eat a few large meals throughout the day. Instead, they instinctively eat little and often, grazing continuously across both daytime and nighttime hours.

This constant eating behaviour is closely tied to how their digestive systems function. Rabbits rely on a steady movement of fibre through the gut, which is why long periods without food can quickly become dangerous. Even a short loss of appetite should always be taken seriously.

Because of this, feeding rabbits is less about strict “meal portions” and more about creating the right balance of foods that remain available consistently throughout the day.

---

## Hay Should Always Be the Main Food Source

Hay is the foundation of a healthy rabbit diet and should make up the vast majority of what a rabbit eats each day. In most cases, around 80–90% of their daily food intake should come from fresh hay alone.

Beyond simply filling them up, hay plays a major role in supporting digestion and naturally wearing down a rabbit’s constantly growing teeth. Rabbits that do not eat enough hay are far more likely to develop digestive problems, dental issues, or unhealthy feeding habits.

Fresh hay should always be available and should never fully run out, even overnight. Many rabbits prefer grazing throughout quieter periods of the day, especially early mornings and evenings.

Good hay should smell fresh, feel dry, and remain free from dust or dampness. Timothy hay, meadow hay, and orchard grass hay are all popular choices for adult rabbits.

---

## Fresh Greens Are Important in Moderation

Alongside hay, rabbits benefit from a daily portion of fresh leafy greens. Vegetables help add variety, enrichment, and additional nutrients to the diet, but they should support hay intake rather than replace it.

A mixture of rabbit-safe greens such as coriander, parsley, romaine lettuce, spring greens, and basil can work well as part of a balanced routine. Offering a variety of leaves often encourages natural foraging behaviour and keeps feeding time more interesting.

That said, vegetables should still be fed thoughtfully. If rabbits fill up too heavily on greens or sugary vegetables, they may begin eating less hay, which can create long-term health issues over time.

Introducing new vegetables slowly is always recommended, especially for younger rabbits or rabbits with sensitive digestion.

---

## Pellets Should Stay Controlled

Pellets are often the easiest part of a rabbit’s diet to accidentally overfeed. Because pellets are calorie-dense and relatively low in fibre compared to hay, rabbits only need a small measured amount each day once fully grown.

Many owners assume pellets should form the centre of the diet, but healthy adult rabbits should rely primarily on hay instead.

The amount of pellets a rabbit needs depends on several factors, including:
- Size
- Breed
- Activity level
- Age
- Overall body condition

Young rabbits typically require more pellets while growing, but healthy adults usually only need a modest portion once per day.

Choosing a high-fibre pellet without colourful pieces or sugary additions is generally the best option. Muesli-style mixes are often avoided because rabbits tend to selectively eat the less nutritious parts first.

---

## Water Intake Matters Just as Much

Hydration plays a major role in keeping a rabbit’s digestive system functioning properly. Because rabbits consume so much dry fibre through hay, access to clean water at all times is essential.

Many rabbits prefer drinking from bowls rather than bottles because it feels more natural and allows them to drink larger amounts more comfortably. Regardless of the setup used, water should always be kept fresh and checked regularly throughout the day.

A rabbit that suddenly drinks far less than usual can sometimes be showing early signs of illness or discomfort.

---

## Feeding Changes Throughout a Rabbit’s Life

A rabbit’s feeding needs naturally change as they grow older.

Young rabbits require more calories and nutrients to support healthy development, which is why pellets are often fed more freely during the early months. Hay still remains extremely important during this stage, helping support digestion and healthy tooth development from a young age.

Adult rabbits usually require a more controlled diet focused heavily around hay, with smaller pellet portions and daily greens helping maintain balance.

Older rabbits may begin eating less overall or show changes in chewing behaviour as they age. Monitoring appetite, weight, and droppings becomes even more important for senior rabbits, as appetite changes are often one of the earliest signs that something may be wrong.

---

## Signs Your Rabbit Is Eating the Right Amount

A healthy rabbit will generally maintain a steady appetite throughout the day and continue grazing on hay regularly between feeding times.

Other good signs include:
- Consistent droppings
- Stable body weight
- Good energy levels
- Interest in food
- Normal behaviour and activity

If a rabbit suddenly becomes less interested in food, especially hay, it should never be ignored. Rabbits can deteriorate surprisingly quickly once digestion slows down.

---

## Common Feeding Mistakes

Many feeding problems come from overcomplicating a rabbit’s diet or relying too heavily on treats and pellets.

Some of the most common mistakes include:
- Feeding too many pellets
- Offering sugary treats too often
- Not providing enough hay
- Changing foods too quickly
- Feeding colourful mixed rabbit foods

Small adjustments to diet can make a significant difference to a rabbit’s long-term health and wellbeing.

---

## Final Thoughts

Feeding rabbits properly is largely about consistency and balance rather than strict measurements. Unlimited hay, controlled pellets, fresh leafy greens, and constant access to clean water create the foundation of a healthy diet.

Once these basics are in place, most rabbits naturally regulate their own eating habits well, helping them stay healthy, active, and comfortable throughout their lives.
